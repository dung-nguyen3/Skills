LO1: Explain the steps of external respiration

The 4 Steps of External Respiration:

Step 1: Ventilation - Movement of air between atmosphere and alveoli

  • Ventilation rate is NOT constant
  • Can be adjusted to meet body's need for O‚ÇÇ uptake and CO‚ÇÇ removal
  • Occurs via bidirectional movement through same pathway

Step 2: Gas Exchange (Pulmonary Diffusion) - Exchange of O‚ÇÇ and CO‚ÇÇ between air in alveoli and blood

  • Mechanism: Diffusion
  • Gases diffuse from high pressure to low pressure
  • CO‚ÇÇ diffuses into alveoli, O‚ÇÇ diffuses out of alveoli

Step 3: Transport - Transport of O‚ÇÇ and CO‚ÇÇ between lungs and tissue via pulmonary and systemic circulation

  • O‚ÇÇ transport: Primarily bound to hemoglobin (HbO‚ÇÇ)
  • CO‚ÇÇ transport: Primarily as bicarbonate (HCO‚ÇÉ‚Åª)

Step 4: Gas Exchange (Tissue Diffusion) - Exchange of O‚ÇÇ and CO‚ÇÇ between blood and tissue

  • O‚ÇÇ moves from blood into tissues
  • CO‚ÇÇ moves from tissues into blood

Step 5: Internal Respiration

  • Location: Mitochondria
  • Process: Glucose + O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí CO‚ÇÇ + H‚ÇÇO + ATP

üí° Clinical Pearl

The respiratory system's primary function is efficient delivery of O‚ÇÇ from air to tissues and removal of CO‚ÇÇ from tissues to air.

LO2: Describe the respiratory quotient (RQ)

RQ = CO‚ÇÇ produced / O‚ÇÇ consumed = VCO‚ÇÇ / VO‚ÇÇ

RQ Values by Substrate:

  • RQ = 0.7 ‚Üí Fats (lipid metabolism)
  • RQ = 0.8 ‚Üí Proteins OR mixed consumption of proteins, fats, and carbohydrates
  • RQ = 1.0 ‚Üí Carbohydrates

Example Calculation (from source):

RQ = (200 mL/min CO‚ÇÇ produced) / (250 mL/min O‚ÇÇ consumed) = 0.8

‚≠ê High-Yield Fact

The RQ value indicates which macronutrient is being primarily metabolized. Lower RQ (0.7) = more fat metabolism, Higher RQ (1.0) = more carbohydrate metabolism.

LO3: Compare and contrast the components and role of the conducting zone and ventilatory zones of the lung

Respiratory System Organization:

Conducting Zone (Anatomical Dead Space ~150 mL)

  • Components:
    • Trachea (Generation 0)
    • Bronchi (Generations 1-4)
    • Bronchioles (Generations 5-16)
    • Terminal bronchioles (Generation 16)
  • Role: Move air into/out of respiratory zone for gas exchange
  • Functions:
    • Humidification and warming of air stream
    • Decreases velocity of airflow
    • Regulation of airway resistance (smooth muscle)
    • Removal of dust (cilia and mucous secreting goblet cells)

Respiratory Zone (Exchange Zone, Alveolar Ventilation Volume)

  • Components:
    • Respiratory bronchioles (Generations 17-19)
    • Alveolar ducts (Generations 20-22)
    • Alveolar sacs (Generation 23)
  • Role: Gas exchange via diffusion
  • Design feature: Increase surface area for diffusion

Alveolar Structure:

  • Type I alveolar cells: Alveolar epithelium (gas exchange surface)
  • Type II alveolar cells: Synthesize pulmonary surfactant
  • Alveolar macrophages: Defense (instead of cilia)
  • Numbers: ~300 million alveoli per lung
  • Diameter: ~200 Œºm per alveolus
  • Total surface area: ~50-100 m¬≤ (tennis court!)

Alveolar-Capillary Interface:

  • Three layers: Alveolar epithelium, interstitial fluid, capillary endothelium
  • Diffusion distance: 0.5 Œºm (very thin!)
  • RBC transit time: ~0.75 seconds in capillary, traversing 2-3 alveoli

üí° Clinical Pearl

The thin barrier (0.5 Œºm) and huge surface area (tennis court) are optimized for rapid gas diffusion according to Fick's Law.

LO4: Discuss the role of the autonomic nervous system in controlling airway resistance

Smooth muscle of bronchi and bronchioles is controlled by the ANS:

Sympathetic Nervous System (SNS):

  • Receptor: Adrenergic Œ≤‚ÇÇ receptors on bronchial smooth muscle
  • Effect: Airway dilation (bronchodilation)
  • Airway resistance: Decreases

Parasympathetic Nervous System (PNS):

  • Receptor: Cholinergic M‚ÇÉ receptors on bronchial smooth muscle
  • Effect: Airway constriction (bronchoconstriction)
  • Airway resistance: Increases

Clinical Connection: Asthma

  • Pathophysiology: Asthma attacks due to bronchoconstriction
  • Associated with chronic inflammatory condition
  • Treatment: Commonly treated with Albuterol (Ventolin)
  • Albuterol is a Œ≤‚ÇÇ adrenergic receptor agonist ‚Üí mimics SNS ‚Üí bronchodilation

‚≠ê High-Yield Fact

Œ≤‚ÇÇ agonists (albuterol) treat asthma by causing bronchodilation and decreasing airway resistance.

LO5: Describe the role of mucociliary clearance in removing foreign particles

Mucociliary Transport System:

Effective clearance requires both ciliary activity AND respiratory tract fluids:

Sol Layer (Periciliary Fluid):

  • Produced by ciliated cells
  • Low viscosity
  • Optimal for ciliary activity
  • Allows cilia to beat effectively

Gel Layer (Mucus):

  • Produced by goblet cells
  • Thickness: 5-10 Œºm, distributed in patches
  • High viscosity and high elastic properties
  • Traps inhaled materials

Clinical Connection: Effects of Smoking

Cigarette smoking causes:

  • Increased thickness of airway walls
  • Increased number of goblet cells (more mucus production)
  • Decreased activity and number of ciliary cells
  • Result: Impaired mucociliary clearance
  • Leads to trapped pathogens in mucus layer

üí° Clinical Pearl

Smoking damages both components of mucociliary clearance: it increases mucus production (more goblet cells) while simultaneously decreasing the ability to clear that mucus (fewer/less active cilia).

LO7: Define partial pressure of a gas

Definition:

Partial pressure of a gas (Pgas) is the pressure a single gas exerts in a mixture of gases.

Atmospheric Composition:

  • Most abundant gas: Nitrogen (78%)
  • Oxygen: 21%
  • Other gases: Argon (0.9%), CO‚ÇÇ (0.037%)
  • Average atmospheric pressure at sea level: ‚âà 760 mmHg (barometric pressure)

LO8: Describe tidal volume and dead space

Tidal Ventilation:

Humans use tidal ventilation - air moves bidirectionally into and out of lungs by the same path.

Tidal Volume (TV):

  • Definition: Amount of air that moves in and out per breath during normal breathing
  • Normal value: ~500 mL per breath
  • Can be adjusted during exercise (along with breathing rate) to meet increased metabolic demand

Lung Volume Components:

  • Inspiratory reserve volume: Additional air that can be inhaled beyond tidal volume
  • Tidal volume: Normal breathing volume (any level of activity)
  • Expiratory reserve volume: Additional air that can be exhaled beyond tidal volume
  • Residual volume: Air remaining in lungs after maximal expiration

Dead Space:

The Challenge with Tidal Breathing:

  • During each breath, the airway (conducting zone) contains ~150 mL of air
  • This 150 mL of "old" air must be inhaled before any fresh inspired air (500 mL) can reach the lungs
  • Dead space is primarily the air in the conducting airways
  • Tidal volume must account for dead space volume to ensure sufficient fresh air reaches alveoli for gas exchange

Example:

  • Inspired breath: 500 mL
  • Dead space: 150 mL
  • Fresh air reaching alveoli: 500 - 150 = 350 mL

üí° Clinical Pearl

Alveolar ventilation (fresh air reaching gas exchange surfaces) = Tidal Volume - Dead Space. This means only ~350 mL of the 500 mL tidal volume actually participates in gas exchange.

‚≠ê Additional High-Yield Information

Functions of the Respiratory System (Beyond Gas Exchange):

  • Regulate pH
  • Vocalization
  • Defense against airborne pathogens - removal of inhaled particles
  • Process blood-borne substances:
    • Angiotensin converting enzyme converts angiotensin I to angiotensin II
    • Enzymes inactivate bradykinin, prostaglandins, and serotonin
    • Certain immunoglobulins (IgA) are released

Pulmonary Vasculature:

  • Pulmonary artery receives all right heart output
  • Pulmonary blood pressure is LOW (arterial pressure ~15 mmHg)
  • Cardiac output still 5-6 L/min ‚Üí Large surface area, low resistance
  • Pulmonary blood flow NOT distributed evenly when standing (due to gravity):
    • Lowest at top of lung
    • Highest at bottom of lung

Key Definitions

External Respiration: The entire process of O‚ÇÇ delivery from atmosphere to tissues and CO‚ÇÇ removal from tissues to atmosphere (5 steps).

Internal Respiration: Cellular respiration occurring in mitochondria where glucose + O‚ÇÇ ‚Üí CO‚ÇÇ + H‚ÇÇO + ATP.

Respiratory Quotient (RQ): Ratio of CO‚ÇÇ produced to O‚ÇÇ consumed; indicates which macronutrient is being primarily metabolized.

Conducting Zone: Airways (trachea ‚Üí terminal bronchioles) that transport and condition air but don't participate in gas exchange; also called anatomical dead space (~150 mL).

Respiratory Zone: Gas exchange regions (respiratory bronchioles ‚Üí alveolar sacs) where diffusion occurs; huge surface area (50-100 m¬≤).

Partial Pressure: The pressure exerted by a single gas in a mixture of gases; calculated as fractional concentration √ó atmospheric pressure.

Tidal Volume: Amount of air moved in and out per breath during normal breathing (~500 mL).

Dead Space: Volume of conducting airways that doesn't participate in gas exchange (~150 mL).

Mucociliary Clearance: Defense mechanism requiring both sol layer (for ciliary beating) and gel layer (mucus to trap particles).
