üìã Learning Objectives & Detailed Answers

Learning Objective: Compare and contrast physiology, clinical findings, diagnostic studies, and treatment plans for the following conditions: hemolytic anemias (G6PD deficiency, Hereditary Spherocytosis, Sickle Cell Anemia, Paroxysmal Nocturnal Hemoglobinuria, Microangiopathic Hemolytic Anemia, Thalassemia), Autoimmune hemolytic anemia, and hemolytic transfusion reaction.

Answer:

Hemolytic anemia is characterized by premature RBC destruction. Normal RBC lifespan is 120 days with 1% loss per day. Bone marrow can compensate for up to 5X increased loss per day, with anemia occurring when greater than 5% loss per day occurs. Hemolysis can be classified as intracorpuscular (cell defect) versus extracorpuscular (environmental), and intravascular versus extravascular (spleen).

ü©∏ Transfusion Testing and Reactions

Coombs Tests:

  • Direct Coombs (DAT - Direct Antiglobulin Test): Tests for antibodies on patient's cells. Detects IgG or complement (C3) on RBCs. May be negative if affected RBCs have lysed.
  • Indirect Coombs (IAT - Indirect Antiglobulin Test): Tests for antibodies in patient's serum. Used for the screen part of "type and screen" and for crossmatch.

Transfusion Preparations:

  • Type and Screen: Type = ABO and D (Rh) testing; Screen = Indirect Coombs/IAT
  • Type and Cross: Type and screen PLUS crossmatch (mixes patient's serum with donor blood cells to confirm compatibility via indirect Coombs/IAT)

üìä Microcytic Hemolytic Anemia: Thalassemia

Overview: Inherited chronic hemolytic anemia protective against malaria. Affects people with ancestry from Mediterranean, Africa, India, Southeast Asia. Caused by abnormality in Œ±-globin or Œ≤-globin chains, leading to dysfunctional chains and premature RBC destruction.

Key Laboratory Features:

  • Proportionately higher than expected RBC count relative to hemoglobin
  • Low MCV, Normal RDW (distinguishes from iron deficiency which has elevated RDW)
  • +/- anemia
  • Iron overload can be a problem
  • Diagnosed with hemoglobin electrophoresis
  • May be identified on newborn heel stick screening

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL Safety Information

  • Acute Hemolytic Transfusion Reaction Emergency: Stop transfusion immediately if fever, flank pain, or dark urine develop. Life-threatening ABO incompatibility.
  • Sickle Cell Acute Chest Syndrome: Fever, chest pain, hypoxia, pulmonary infiltrates - can rapidly progress. Aggressive hydration, analgesia, oxygen, incentive spirometry, consider transfusion.
  • G6PD Crisis: Avoid oxidative triggers. Remember fava beans, sulfonamides, primaquine, dapsone. Can cause severe hemolysis requiring transfusion.
  • Thalassemia Iron Overload: Do NOT give iron supplements unless truly iron deficient. Transfusion-dependent patients develop iron overload requiring chelation.

üíé Clinical Pearls

  • Splenectomy Utility: Curative for Hereditary Spherocytosis, helpful for Thalassemia, NOT helpful for G6PD deficiency
  • DAT Interpretation: Positive in Warm AIHA (IgG), may be positive in Cold AIHA, NEGATIVE in PNH despite being hemolytic
  • Schistocytes Think: MAHA - mechanical heart valves, TTP, HUS, DIC, march hemoglobinuria
  • Heinz Bodies Think: G6PD deficiency with oxidative stress
  • Tea-Colored Urine at Night Think: Paroxysmal Nocturnal Hemoglobinuria
  • Howell-Jolly Bodies Think: Autosplenectomy (sickle cell) or post-splenectomy - vaccinate for encapsulated organisms!
  • Most Common Transfusion Reaction: Febrile non-hemolytic (temperature ‚â•1¬∞C above baseline)
  • MCV Normal, RDW Normal Think: Thalassemia trait (vs iron deficiency which has elevated RDW)
  • Sickle Cell Prophylaxis: PCN until age 5, vaccines for encapsulated organisms, folate for life, hydroxyurea starting at 9 months

üéØ Diagnostic Test Selection

üéØ Which Test to Order

  • Suspect G6PD: G6PD level (may be falsely normal during acute hemolysis - recheck later)
  • Suspect Hereditary Spherocytosis: Osmotic Fragility Test or Flow cytometry
  • Suspect PNH: Flow cytometry (definitive), DAT will be NEGATIVE
  • Suspect Autoimmune: Direct Antiglobulin Test (DAT/Direct Coombs) - positive IgG for warm, may be positive for cold
  • Suspect Sickle Cell or Thalassemia: Hemoglobin electrophoresis
  • Before Transfusion: Type and Screen (routine) or Type and Cross (urgent or if antibodies present)
  • Peripheral Smear Critical For: MAHA (schistocytes), G6PD (Heinz bodies/bite cells), spherocytosis (spherocytes), sickle cell (sickle cells)
