Learning Objective 1: Recognize the major antifungal drugs and recall whether they are useful against systemic or local fungal infections.

Answer:

The major antifungal drugs are divided into two main categories:

SYSTEMIC antifungals:

  • Amphotericin B (Fungizone) - DOC for most systemic infections
  • Flucytosine (Ancobon) - Often combined with amphotericin B
  • Azoles: Ketoconazole, Fluconazole, Voriconazole, Itraconazole
  • Echinocandins: Caspofungin, Micafungin, Anidulafungin

LOCAL/TOPICAL antifungals:

  • Terbinafine (Lamisil) - Onychomycosis
  • Griseofulvin (Fulvicin) - Dermatophytosis
  • Nystatin (Mycostatin) - Candida infections
  • OTC: Clotrimazole, Miconazole, Tolnaftate

üéØ High-Yield Classification

  • Systemic fungi require systemic drugs - life-threatening infections need IV/oral agents
  • Superficial fungi can use topical drugs - skin/nail infections managed with local therapy
  • Some drugs work both ways: Azoles can be systemic or topical

Learning Objective 2: Discuss amphotericin B and be familiar with the drug's toxicity, mechanism of action, and antifungal spectrum

Answer:

Mechanism of Action:

Amphotericin B is a polyene antifungal that binds to ergosterol in fungal cell membranes, forming pores that cause:

  • Membrane depolarization
  • Increased permeability to proteins and cations (K+, Na+, Ca2+)
  • Leakage of intracellular contents ‚Üí cell death

Antifungal Spectrum (Broad):

  • Histoplasma capsulatum
  • Cryptococcus neoformans
  • Coccidioides immitis
  • Candida species
  • Blastomyces dermatitidis
  • Some Aspergillus

Toxicity:

‚ö†Ô∏è Major Toxicities

  • NEPHROTOXICITY - dose-dependent, occurs in nearly all patients
  • Azotemia - elevated BUN and serum creatinine
  • Infusion-related: chills (50%), fever (20%), muscle spasms
  • Hypokalemia, hepatic failure (rare)
  • Management: Hydration critical, slow infusion rate

Learning Objective 3: Discuss why amphotericin B is the drug of first choice for most systemic infections, and why other antifungals might be used in combination.

Answer:

Why Amphotericin B is DOC:

  • Broadest spectrum of any antifungal
  • Fungicidal (kills fungi) rather than fungistatic
  • Effective against most life-threatening systemic mycoses
  • IV administration for severe infections
  • Decades of clinical experience

Combination Therapy Rationales:

  • Flucytosine + Amphotericin B: Synergistic effect, especially for Cryptococcus infections (DOC combination)
  • Voriconazole + Amphotericin B: DOC combination for invasive Aspergillus
  • Combinations can allow lower doses, reducing toxicity
  • Prevent emergence of resistance
  • Broader coverage in empiric therapy

Limitations requiring alternatives:

  • Poor CNS, eye, and bone penetration
  • Severe nephrotoxicity may require switching
  • 6 weeks to 4 months treatment duration

üíé Clinical Pearl

Amphotericin B's broad spectrum makes it ideal for empiric therapy in neutropenic fever when fungal infection is suspected but organism unknown

Learning Objective 4: Discuss flucytosine and be familiar with the drug's toxicity, mechanism of action, and antifungal spectrum.

Answer:

Mechanism of Action:

Flucytosine is converted to 5-fluorouracil (5-FU) inside fungal cells:

  • Cytosine permease transports drug into cell
  • Cytosine deaminase converts to 5-FU
  • 5-FU disrupts both RNA and DNA synthesis
  • Inhibits thymidylate synthase ‚Üí blocks DNA synthesis
  • Substitutes for uracil ‚Üí inhibits protein synthesis

Antifungal Spectrum (Narrower than Ampho B):

  • Cryptococcus - primary use
  • Candida
  • Aspergillus

Toxicity:

  • Bone marrow depression - anemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia
  • GI disturbances
  • Elevated liver enzymes (ALT/AST) - reversible
  • Renal elimination - impairment leads to toxicity

üéØ Must-Know Fact

Rarely used alone due to rapid resistance development - almost always combined with amphotericin B

Learning Objective 5: Specify which fungal infection flucytosine is the DOC for, and what pharmacokinetic property of flucytosine is related to this clinical use.

Answer:

DOC Indication:

Flucytosine + Amphotericin B = DOC for Cryptococcal meningitis

Key Pharmacokinetic Property:

Excellent CNS penetration - enters CSF and aqueous humor

This CSF penetration is critical because:

  • Cryptococcus commonly causes meningitis in immunocompromised patients
  • Many antifungals have poor CNS penetration
  • Flucytosine achieves therapeutic levels in CSF
  • Synergistic with amphotericin B for cryptococcal infections

Administration:

  • Oral administration
  • Renal elimination - dose adjustment needed in renal impairment

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Point

Renal function must be monitored - accumulation in renal failure increases bone marrow toxicity

Learning Objective 6: Discuss the "azole" class of antifungals and recall the drugs in this class.

Answer:

Azole Class Members:

  • Ketoconazole (Nizoral) - rarely used orally due to toxicity
  • Fluconazole (Diflucan) - DOC for valley fever
  • Voriconazole (Vfend) - DOC for Aspergillus
  • Itraconazole (Sporanox) - broad spectrum

Common Mechanism:

All azoles inhibit 14-alpha-demethylase (a cytochrome P450 enzyme), blocking ergosterol synthesis

General Properties:

  • Fungistatic (stop growth) rather than fungicidal
  • Oral and/or IV formulations available
  • Significant drug interactions via P450 inhibition
  • Generally less toxic than amphotericin B

üéØ Remember the Pattern

All end in "-azole" and work by blocking ergosterol synthesis

Learning Objective 8: Discuss the echinocandin class of antifungals and recall the drugs in this class.

Answer:

Echinocandin Class Members:

  • Caspofungin (Cancidas) - first approved
  • Micafungin (Mycamine)
  • Anidulafungin (Eraxis)

Common Features:

  • All are IV only - no oral formulations
  • Called "penicillins of antifungals" - target cell wall
  • Long half-lives (14-50 hours)
  • Hepatic elimination
  • Generally well-tolerated

üéØ Memory Aid

All end in "-fungin" and are fungicidal (kill fungi)

Learning Objective 9: Recognize that the echinocandin group of antifungals share common features and mechanism of action, and identify their adverse side effects and clinical use (DOC).

Answer:

Mechanism of Action:

Inhibits Œ≤(1,3)-D-glucan synthase ‚Üí disrupts fungal cell wall synthesis ‚Üí fungicidal

  • Œ≤-glucan is essential component of fungal cell wall
  • Not present in mammalian cells - selective toxicity

Learning Objective 10: Discuss griseofulvin and be familiar with the drug's toxicity, mechanism of action, antifungal spectrum and clinical use. Discuss why the drug's binding to keratin is advantageous.

Answer:

Mechanism of Action:

  • Binds to microtubules and destroys mitotic spindle
  • Fungistatic - prevents cell division

Antifungal Spectrum & Use:

  • Oral administration
  • Systemic treatment of dermatophytosis (tinea infections)
  • Active against dermatophytes only

Keratin Binding Advantage:

Griseofulvin concentrates in keratin - high levels in:

  • Stratum corneum
  • Outermost epidermis
  • Hair and nails

This prevents infection in new skin structures as they grow, allowing replacement of infected keratin over 6-12 months

Toxicity:

‚ö†Ô∏è Serious Adverse Effects

  • Disulfiram-like effect with alcohol
  • Photosensitivity
  • Hepatotoxicity
  • Teratogenic - contraindicated in pregnancy and men 6 months before fathering
  • Reduces warfarin activity (mechanism unclear)

üíé Clinical Pearl

Long treatment (6-12 months) needed because drug only protects new keratin growth - must wait for infected tissue to be replaced

Learning Objective 11: Discuss terbinafine and be familiar with the drug's toxicity, mechanism of action, antifungal spectrum and clinical use.

Answer:

Mechanism of Action:

  • Inhibits squalene monooxygenase/epoxidase
  • Blocks conversion of squalene to lanosterol
  • Squalene accumulation is toxic to fungi ‚Üí fungal cell death
  • Different target than azoles (earlier in ergosterol pathway)

Clinical Use:

  • Oral or topical formulations
  • Superior to griseofulvin for onychomycosis
  • Well absorbed, concentrates within nail
  • Fungicidal against dermatophytes

Antifungal Spectrum:

  • Dermatophytes (primary use)
  • Onychomycosis (nail infections)
  • Tinea infections

Toxicity:

  • Hepatotoxicity - monitor liver function
  • Smell and taste disturbances - unique side effect

üéØ Key Advantage

Shorter treatment duration than griseofulvin and better nail penetration make it preferred for onychomycosis

Learning Objective 12: Discuss nystatin and be familiar with the drug's toxicity, mechanism of action, antifungal spectrum and clinical use.

Answer:

Mechanism of Action:

  • Polyene antibiotic (similar to amphotericin B)
  • Binds to ergosterol ‚Üí alters membrane permeability
  • Causes leakage of small molecules ‚Üí cell death

Clinical Use:

Primarily used for Candida infections:

  • Oral thrush: Oral suspension ("swish and swallow")
  • Intestinal candidiasis: Oral tablets
  • Skin infections: Creams, powders, ointments
  • Vaginal candidiasis: Vaginal tablets

Antifungal Spectrum:

  • Candida (primary target)
  • Cryptococcus
  • Histoplasma
  • Blastomyces
  • Various dermatophytes

Pharmacokinetics & Toxicity:

  • Not absorbed from skin, mucous membranes, or GI tract
  • Acts locally only - "too toxic for systemic use"
  • Very well tolerated topically/orally
  • Mild GI upset (nausea, diarrhea) possible with oral use

üíé Clinical Pearl

Nystatin's lack of absorption makes it perfect for superficial infections but useless for systemic disease - "what you see is what you treat"
