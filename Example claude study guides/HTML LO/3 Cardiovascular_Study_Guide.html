Learning Objective 1: Demonstrate proper utilization and placement of the stethoscope, cardiac inspection, and palpation.

Answer:

üéß Stethoscope Anatomy & Placement

Earpieces point towards your EYES - This is critical for proper sound transmission.

Chestpiece Functions:

  • DIAPHRAGM (Firm pressure): HIGH frequency sounds - S1, S2, most heart sounds
  • BELL (Light pressure): LOW frequency sounds - S3, S4, tricuspid stenosis, mitral stenosis

üíé Clinical Pearl

  • Testing Stethoscope: After putting earpieces in (pointing toward eyes), tap the side of chestpiece you're using to ensure it's rotated correctly
  • Modesty: Only place stethoscope directly on skin you can completely see. For standardized patient exams, it's OK to place on top of sports bra or shorts

ü´Ä Cardiac Auscultation Landmarks

Mnemonic: "All Patients Trust Me"

Position Location Valve(s) Heard Key Sounds
Aortic (A) Right 2nd ICS Aortic valve S2 > S1, Aortic stenosis
Pulmonic (P) Left 2nd ICS Pulmonic valve S2 > S1, Pulmonic stenosis, S2 split
Tricuspid (T) Left 4th ICS Tricuspid valve Tricuspid regurgitation, VSD
Mitral (M) 5th ICS midclavicular line Mitral valve, PMI/Apex S1 > S2, Mitral regurgitation, MVP
Erb's Point (2nd P) Left 3rd ICS Multiple valves Good for overall assessment

üëÅÔ∏è Cardiac Inspection & Palpation

Patient Position: 30¬∞ elevation

Examiner Position: Stand on patient's right side

Inspection - Look for:

  • Abnormal movements (heaves/lifts)
  • Point of maximal impulse (PMI)
  • Scars, bruising, swelling

Palpation - Palpate lightly to detect:

  • PMI/Apical impulse: Normally at 5th ICS midclavicular line, size of quarter
  • Thrills: Purr-like vibrations from abnormal blood flow
  • Heaves/lifts: Lifting movement under anterior chest

üéØ High-Yield Exam Info

  • PMI Location: 5th ICS midclavicular line = mitral area = apex
  • Displaced PMI: Suggests cardiomegaly or COPD (laterally displaced, spans larger area)
  • Finding Sternal Angle: Use chopsticks to identify angle change, palpate sternal angle (Angle of Louis), rib #2 slightly below, 2nd ICS just below that

Learning Objective 2: Determine when to use the bell or diaphragm when conducting a cardiac physical exam.

Answer:

Diaphragm (Firm Pressure): HIGH frequency (pitch) sounds

  • S1, S2 (normal heart sounds)
  • Most murmurs
  • Pericardial friction rub

Bell (Light Pressure): LOW frequency (pitch) sounds

  • S3, S4 (diastolic filling sounds)
  • Mitral stenosis
  • Tricuspid stenosis
  • Still's murmur (innocent)
  • Venous hum (innocent)

üéØ High-Yield Exam Info

  • Tunable Diaphragm: Light pressure = bell (low pitch); Firm pressure = diaphragm (high pitch)
  • Which Heart Sounds with Bell? S3, S4, mitral stenosis, tricuspid stenosis, Still's murmur, venous hum

Learning Objective 3: Explain the mechanism for different types of heart sounds: S1, S2, S3, S4, split S2 (normal, wide, fixed wide, and paradoxical), mitral valve prolapse, cardiac friction rub.

Answer:

üîä Normal Heart Sounds

We hear valves CLOSING, not blood flow

Sound Mechanism Timing Loudest Location Key Info
S1 ("Lub") AV valves CLOSE (Mitral & Tricuspid) Beginning of SYSTOLE S1 > S2 at APEX/Mitral Pulse occurs right after S1
S2 ("Dub") Semilunar valves CLOSE (Aortic & Pulmonic) Beginning of DIASTOLE S2 > S1 at BASE Aortic closes before pulmonic

üéµ S2 Splitting Patterns

Type of Split Expiration Inspiration Etiology
Normal (Physiologic) Single S2 Wider split Normal finding, easier to hear with slower heart rates
Wide Split Already split Even wider Pulmonic stenosis (delayed P2)
Fixed Wide Split Wide split NO change ASD, RV overload
Paradoxical Split Split present Split disappears Left bundle branch block (delayed A2)

üéØ High-Yield S2 Split Mechanism

  • Normal Split: Inspiration ‚Üí increased RV venous return ‚Üí longer RV ejection time ‚Üí pulmonic valve closes even later
  • Why P2 after A2 normally? Left ventricle empties earlier (more muscular wall)
  • Fixed Split (ASD): Not affected by inspiration - pathognomonic finding

üé∂ Diastolic Filling Sounds (Extra Heart Sounds)

S3 Gallop - "Sloshing into a Waltz"

  • Mechanism: Blood rushing into ventricle during 1st phase of diastolic filling
  • Pitch: LOW (use bell)
  • Normal: Children, young adults, last trimester pregnancy (compliant ventricle)
  • Pathologic: Dilated ventricle, decreased contractility, heart failure, volume overload

S4 Gallop - "A Stiff Wall"

  • Mechanism: Blood hitting stiff left ventricle during 2nd phase of diastolic filling
  • Pitch: LOW (use bell)
  • Normal: Athletes
  • Pathologic: Ventricular hypertrophy or fibrosis

üíé Clinical Pearl - Remembering S3 vs S4

  • S3 = "Sloshing": Think of liquid sloshing into a baggy, dilated ventricle (heart failure)
  • S4 = "Stiff": Blood hitting a thick, stiff wall (hypertrophy)
  • Both are LOW pitch: Always use bell/light pressure

üéµ Other Heart Sounds

Mitral Valve Prolapse (MVP)

  • Sound: Midsystolic CLICK + possible late systolic murmur
  • Mechanism: Floppy mitral valve prolapses into left atrium, often with mitral regurgitation
  • Position: Best heard left lateral decubitus at mitral area
  • Maneuvers: Accentuated by standing/Valsalva

Pericardial Friction Rub

  • Sound: Coarse grating/sandpaper sound
  • Mechanism: Inflammation of visceral and parietal pericardium rubbing together
  • Components: Can have 3 sounds (atrial systole, ventricular systole, rapid ventricular filling)
  • Position: Best heard left parasternal 3rd ICS with patient leaning forward, holding breath after forced exhalation

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL Sound Patterns

  • S3 in older adults: Think heart failure until proven otherwise
  • S4: Think ventricular hypertrophy/stiffness
  • Fixed split S2: Pathognomonic for ASD
  • Paradoxical split: Think left bundle branch block
  • Pericardial friction rub: Emergency - pericarditis

Learning Objective 4: For the following conditions, describe the exam findings including timing, auscultation location, shape, characteristics, and if there are maneuvers to manipulate the sound: Aortic stenosis, Mitral regurgitation, mitral stenosis, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, patent ductus arteriosus, ventricular septal defect, and mitral valve prolapse.

Answer:

üéº Murmur Fundamentals

What is a murmur? Loud turbulent blood flow through:

  • Narrow valves (stenosis)
  • Leaking valves (regurgitation)
  • Defects (VSD, PDA)

üéØ High-Yield Murmur Rules

  • Stenosis of AV valves: DIASTOLIC murmur (blood moving forward through narrow valve during diastole)
  • Stenosis of Semilunar valves: SYSTOLIC murmur (blood moving forward through narrow valve during systole)
  • Regurgitation of AV valves: SYSTOLIC murmur (blood moving backward during systole)
  • Regurgitation of Semilunar valves: DIASTOLIC murmur (blood moving backward during diastole)

üîä Murmur Grading (1-6)

Grade Description
Grade 1 Barely audible in quiet room
Grade 2 Quiet, but immediately audible
Grade 3 Moderately loud
Grade 4 Loud and associated with THRILL
Grade 5 Very loud, thrill easily palpable
Grade 6 Very loud, thrill palpable, audible without stethoscope touching chest

  • If radiates to NECK: Think Aortic Stenosis
  • If radiates to LEFT AXILLA: Think Mitral Regurgitation
  • Opening snap after S2: Pathognomonic for Mitral Stenosis
  • "Machinery" murmur: Think Patent Ductus Arteriosus
  • Very loud with thrill in child: Think VSD

üéØ High-Yield Maneuver Pattern

HCM and MVP behave OPPOSITE to other murmurs:

  • HCM & MVP: Worsen with ‚Üì preload (standing/Valsalva), improve with ‚Üë preload (squatting)
  • All other murmurs: Improve with ‚Üì preload, worsen with ‚Üë preload
  • Why? HCM causes subaortic narrowing - less blood = more obstruction; MVP - less blood = floppy valve prolapses earlier

  • Right-sided murmurs (TR, TS, PR, PS): Increase with INSPIRATION
  • Why? Inspiration increases venous return to right heart ‚Üí more blood flow ‚Üí louder murmur
  • Mnemonic: "Right side murmurs like insPIRation" (P = Pulmonic, I = Inspiration, R = Right side)

Learning Objective 6: Define JVD and JVP and demonstrate measurement of JVP.

Answer:

üîç Jugular Venous Pressure (JVP) Concepts

JVP (Jugular Venous Pressure): Height of internal jugular venous pulsation from sternal angle + 5cm

JVD (Jugular Venous Distension): ELEVATED JVP (>8cm) - This is ABNORMAL

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL Concept

  • Why Measure JVP? The internal jugular vein provides a direct window to right atrial pressure
  • Clinical Significance: Elevated JVP suggests right heart failure, fluid overload, or cardiac dysfunction

üìè How to Measure JVP

Patient Position: 30¬∞ elevation

Steps:

  1. Have patient look to the LEFT with relaxed sternocleidomastoid
  2. Look for the internal jugular vein (lateral to carotid, between earlobe and medial clavicle)
  3. Use tangential light to help visualization
  4. Measure vertical height from sternal angle to top of venous pulsation
  5. Add 5cm to this measurement = JVP

üéØ High-Yield Formula

JVP = Height from sternal angle + 5cm

JVD = JVP >8cm (or height from sternal angle >3cm)

Example: If internal jugular pulsation is 4cm above sternal angle ‚Üí JVP = 4 + 5 = 9cm ‚Üí This is JVD (abnormal)

üîé Internal Jugular Vein vs Carotid Artery

Mnemonic: "POLICE"

Feature Internal Jugular Vein Carotid Artery
Palpation NOT palpable Palpable
Occlusion Easily occluded Not easily occluded
Location Lateral to carotid Medial position
Inspiration Decreases with inspiration No change
Contour Biphasic (2 pulsations/heartbeat) Single pulsation
Erect position Decreases when upright No change

üíé Clinical Pearl

  • Right Side Preferred: Right internal jugular vein has direct path to right atrium (no intervening valves)
  • JVP Physiology: The 2-part wave ("a" = atrial contraction, "v" = maximum atrial filling) corresponds to cardiac cycle
  • If JVP Not Visible at 30¬∞: Patient may need to be more upright (45-90¬∞) if elevated, or more supine (15¬∞) if low

Learning Objective 7: Demonstrate a correct and detailed cardiovascular physical exam and the pertinent specialized tests.

Answer:

üìã Complete Cardiovascular Exam Sequence

Patient Position: 30¬∞ elevation

Examiner: Stand on patient's right side

üîç Step-by-Step Exam

  1. Inspection (Patient at 30¬∞):
    • Abnormal movements, heaves/lifts
    • Point of maximal impulse (PMI)
    • Scars, bruising, swelling
    • JVD - patient turning head left
  2. Palpation (Patient at 30¬∞):
    • Palpate base, left sternal border, apex
    • Assess for thrills, heaves/lifts, PMI/apical impulse
  3. Auscultation (Patient at 30¬∞, then seated):
    • Listen at 4 areas (A, P, T, M) with bell and diaphragm
    • Use light and firm pressure
    • Note: Rate, rhythm, S1, S2, extra sounds, murmurs

üéØ High-Yield Specialized Test Summary

  • JVP Measurement: Height from sternal angle + 5cm = JVP; >8cm = JVD (abnormal)
  • Hepatojugular Reflux: ‚Üë >3cm = positive = right heart dysfunction
  • Best positions for murmurs: Leaning forward (AR, friction rub), Left lateral decubitus (MS, MVP, S3, S4)
  • Allen Test: Negative test (normal) = flushing <5 seconds

üíé Clinical Pearl - Practical Exam Tips

  • Always state what you're doing: "I am inspecting for apical impulse, PMI, and JVD"
  • Ensure good lighting: Critical for visualizing JVP
  • Warm stethoscope: Improves patient comfort and cooperation
  • Draping: Maintain patient modesty throughout exam

  • Hepatojugular reflux: Positive if ‚Üë >3cm
