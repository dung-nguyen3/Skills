Learning Objective 1: Be able to discuss the life cycle of HIV, and recognize that HIV is a retrovirus (RNA) that requires reverse transcriptase to form DNA which can then integrate into host DNA.

Answer:

HIV is a retrovirus (RNA virus) that requires reverse transcriptase to replicate. The HIV life cycle consists of seven critical steps:

1. Binding (Attachment): HIV binds to CD4 cell receptors and co-receptors (CCR5 or CXCR4)

2. Fusion: HIV envelope and CD4 membrane fuse, allowing viral entry

3. Reverse Transcription: HIV releases reverse transcriptase to convert viral RNA into HIV DNA

4. Integration: HIV uses integrase enzyme to insert viral DNA into host cell DNA

5. Replication: HIV uses CD4 cell machinery to make long chains of HIV proteins

6. Assembly: New viral proteins and HIV RNA move to cell surface and assemble

7. Budding: Newly formed immature HIV pushes out of host CD4 cell, releases protease for maturation

üéØ High-Yield: Drug Target Points

  • Entry/Fusion: CCR5 antagonists (maraviroc), fusion inhibitors (enfuvirtide)
  • Reverse Transcription: NRTIs and NNRTIs
  • Integration: Integrase inhibitors (dolutegravir)
  • Maturation: Protease inhibitors (darunavir)

üíé Clinical Pearl

Key distinction: HIV is an RNA virus that must convert its genetic material to DNA using reverse transcriptase - this unique step is absent in human cells, making it an ideal drug target.

Learning Objective 2: Recognize that the treatment of HIV requires multiple antiretroviral drugs.

Answer:

HIV treatment requires combination therapy known as HAART (Highly Active Antiretroviral Therapy) to prevent resistance and achieve viral suppression.

Standard HAART Regimen for Treatment-Naive Patients:

  • Two nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors (NRTIs) PLUS
  • One additional drug from:
    • An integrase strand transfer inhibitor (INSTI), OR
    • A non-nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitor (NNRTI), OR
    • A protease inhibitor (PI) + pharmacoenhancer

Why Combination Therapy is Essential:

  • Decreases viral load more effectively
  • Prevents or delays resistance development
  • Can produce remission in some patients
  • Drugs do NOT eradicate the virus - lifelong therapy required

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL: Monotherapy is Never Used

  • Rapid resistance develops with single drug therapy
  • Combination therapy is the standard of care
  • Pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP): Daily HIV medicines for high-risk individuals
  • Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP): Must start within 72 hours of exposure

Learning Objective 5: Be familiar with the NNRTIs, and how their mechanism of action is different than NRTIs.

Answer:

NNRTIs work by a completely different mechanism than NRTIs:

Feature NRTIs NNRTIs

Block enzyme activity

NNRTI Drugs:

  • Rilpivirine (Edurant)
  • Doravirine (Pifeltro)

üéØ Key Mechanism Difference

NNRTIs bind to a hydrophobic pocket near the active site of reverse transcriptase, causing conformational change that blocks DNA polymerization. They do NOT need to be incorporated into the growing DNA chain like NRTIs.


üß† Memory Trick - "NRTIs vs NNRTIs":

  • NRTIs = "Needs"
    • Nucleotide analogue
    • Enters DNA chain
    • Ends chain (termination)
    • Depends on phosphorylation
    • Slow resistance
  • NNRTIs = "No Need"
    • No phosphorylation required
    • No incorporation into DNA
    • Non-competitive inhibition
    • Easy (rapid) resistance
    • External binding site
    • Direct enzyme binding

üíé Clinical Pearl

NNRTIs must always be used in combination with NRTIs - rapid resistance develops if used alone.

Learning Objective 7: Recall the drugs that comprise the Protease Inhibitor class of antiretrovirals, and their common mechanism of action.

Answer:

Protease Inhibitor Drugs:

  • Darunavir (Prezista) - Drug of first choice when PI included
  • Atazanavir (Reyataz) - Drug of second choice
  • Ritonavir (Norvir) - Pharmacoenhancer
  • Cobicistat (Tybost) - Pharmacoenhancer

üéØ Common Mechanism of Action

MOA: PIs bind to HIV protease enzyme, inhibiting its function of digesting long viral polypeptides into smaller, mature functional proteins. Without protease activity:

  • Viral particles cannot mature
  • Individual core proteins are not released
  • Viruses remain non-infectious

üíé Key Concept

Protease inhibitors work at the final step of viral maturation - preventing the conversion of immature viral particles into infectious virions.

Learning Objective 8: Recognize the common adverse effects and common pharmacokinetics of PIs and the potential for drug interactions that will influence the metabolism of protease inhibitors.

Answer:

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Adverse Effects - All PIs

  • Altered body fat distribution: Buffalo hump, truncal obesity, facial/peripheral atrophy
  • Insulin resistance and hyperglycemia
  • Increased serum cholesterol (use pitavastatin or pravastatin if statin needed)
  • Spontaneous bleeding in hemophilia A or B patients

üß† Memory Trick - PI Side Effects "BICS":

  • Buffalo hump & body fat changes
  • Insulin resistance (diabetes)
  • Cholesterol elevation
  • Spontaneous bleeding

üéØ Common Pharmacokinetics

  • Route: Oral administration
  • All PIs are metabolized by CYP3A4
  • Often given with ritonavir or cobicistat (CYP3A4 inhibitors) to:
    • Increase PI blood levels
    • Allow lower doses
    • Less frequent dosing
  • Mutations and resistance common if used alone - must combine with NRTIs

üß† Memory Tricks for PIs:

  • "All -navirs end in NAVIR" (easy to identify PIs)
  • "DARUN away from sulfa" - Darunavir contraindicated with sulfa allergy
  • "RITA boosts" - Ritonavir is the booster
  • "St. John's WARTS your treatment" - St. John's Wort ruins (warts) PI therapy
  • Remember PI metabolism: "3A4 PIs" = CYP3A4 metabolizes all PIs
Drug Interaction Type Effect on PIs Examples
CYP3A4 Inducers Decrease PI levels Rifampin, phenytoin, phenobarbital, carbamazepine
CYP3A4 Inhibitors Increase PI levels Ritonavir, cobicistat (used therapeutically)
Contraindicated Severe interaction St. John's Wort - NEVER use (increases PI metabolism)

üíé Clinical Pearl

Drug-to-drug interactions are a key challenge in HIV/TB co-infection, as TB treatments (especially rifampin) can significantly impact effectiveness of HIV medicines.

Learning Objective 9: Describe the use of ritonavir (Norvir) in HIV therapy, and why it should (or shouldn't) be prescribed with other PIs.

Answer:

Ritonavir has a unique role as a pharmacokinetic enhancer or "booster" in HIV therapy.

üéØ Why Ritonavir SHOULD Be Used with Other PIs

  • MOA: Potent CYP3A4 inhibitor
  • Effect: Increases bioavailability of other PIs by decreasing their metabolism
  • Benefits:
    • Permits less frequent dosing (once daily instead of multiple times)
    • Allows lower doses of the primary PI
    • Greater antiviral activity
    • Improves patient compliance

  • Adverse effects: GI (vomiting, weakness, diarrhea), burning, tingling, elevated liver enzymes
  • Multiple drug interactions due to CYP3A4 inhibition

Learning Objective 10: Discuss why you wouldn't use two PIs in individuals with sulfonamide hypersensitivities.

Answer:

Darunavir (Prezista) contains a sulfonamide moiety and must not be used in patients with sulfonamide allergy.

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL: Sulfonamide Contraindication

Darunavir (Prezista):

  • Contains sulfonamide structure
  • Absolutely contraindicated in sulfa-allergic patients
  • Can cause severe hypersensitivity reactions
  • This is the primary PI of choice, so alternatives must be used

Note: The source material doesn't specify which other PI contains sulfonamide, but darunavir is clearly identified as having this issue. In sulfa-allergic patients, atazanavir would be the alternative PI choice.

üéØ Key Concept: Entry/Fusion Inhibition

These drugs prevent HIV from entering CD4 cells by blocking different steps:

  • Co-receptor binding: Maraviroc (CCR5)
  • Fusion: Enfuvirtide (gp41)

  • Ibalizumab - CD4

üíé Clinical Use Pattern

Fusion inhibitors are typically reserved for treatment-experienced patients with resistance to other drug classes, not first-line therapy.

Learning Objective 13: Discuss the mechanism of action for Dolutegravir, an integrase inhibitor, and its importance in treating treatment-resistant patients.

Answer:

Integrase Inhibitors:

  • Dolutegravir (Tivicay)
  • Cabotegravir (Vocabria)
  • Bictegravir

üéØ Mechanism of Action - Dolutegravir

MOA: Blocks the integrase enzyme needed for replication by inhibiting the strand transfer step of viral DNA integration into host cell DNA.

  • Prevents viral DNA from becoming part of host chromosome
  • Viral DNA remains linear and unintegrated
  • Cannot produce new viral proteins
  • Typically used in combination with NRTIs
